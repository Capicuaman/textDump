4. **Identify the three key minerals and their specific roles**
   - **Sodium:** Allows muscle cells to contract and relax (primary electrolyte)
   - **Potassium:** Regulates fluid inside muscle cells; deficiency = weakness + cramping
   - **Magnesium:** Critical for muscle relaxation; deficiency = involuntary contractions
   - Visual aid suggestion: Simple 3-box diagram showing roles

5. **Explain why this happens MORE during certain activities**
   - Intense exercise increases sweat rate (more mineral loss)
   - Heat + humidity magnify the problem
   - Longer workouts = more cumulative loss
   - Some athletes are "heavy sweaters" (lose minerals faster)

6. **Connect to the reader's experience**

   - "Most athletes don't realize they're losing more minerals in one 60-minute workout than they replenish with a bottle of water."

> This is why cramping often happens AFTER a workout, not during. Your sweat losses accumulate. Your mineral reserves deplete. Then, when you go to move or stretch, your muscles cramp because they've lost the chemical signal to relax.

   - This is why people feel worse after drinking more water—it's actually diluting them further

5. **The inflammation piece**
   - Alcohol triggers inflammation in the body
   - Electrolytes (especially sodium) help regulate inflammatory response
   - Low electrolytes = amplified inflammation = worse hangover
   - This is why electrolyte replacement reduces symptoms faster than time alone

6. **The blood sugar crash**

   - Dehydration makes it worse
   - Low blood sugar = nausea, fatigue, weakness
   - Electrolytes help stabilize blood sugar (indirectly, through metabolic support)

7. **The sleep disruption piece**

   - You wake up "less rested" even after 8 hours
   - Dehydration worsens sleep quality
   - Electrolyte loss impairs recovery during sleep
   - Morning-after fatigue is partly from poor-quality sleep

**Q3: "Can I mix electrolytes with coffee/caffeine?"**

**Q4: "Do I have to eat anything after electrolytes?"**

**Q5: "Will electrolytes work if I wait 12 hours after drinking?"**
> Yes, but more effective within the first 6 hours. If it's been 12 hours, your body has already started rebalancing on its own. Electrolytes will still help, but the benefit is smaller. Better to take them the next morning immediately upon waking.

**Q6: "Are electrolytes bad for you if you take them when you don't have a hangover?"**
