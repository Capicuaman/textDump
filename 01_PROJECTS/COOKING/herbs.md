The "Foundational Five" - The Most Common & Versatile

These herbs are a great starting point for any kitchen pantry. They are widely used in Western cuisines but appear globally.

1. Basil

· Flavor: Sweet, slightly peppery, and aromatic with a clove-like hint.
· Common Uses:
  · Italian: The star of pesto, essential for Caprese salad (with tomato and mozzarella), and on pizzas and pastas.
  · Thai & Southeast Asian: Thai Basil (which has a licorice note) is used in curries, stir-fries, and dishes like Pad Krapow.
  · General: Brilliant with tomatoes, in salads, and to garnish soups.
· Tip: Add at the very end of cooking, as heat diminishes its flavor.

2. Parsley (Flat-Leaf & Curly)

· Flavor: Fresh, grassy, and slightly peppery. Flat-leaf (Italian) parsley has a more robust flavor, while curly parsley is milder and often used as a garnish.
· Common Uses:
  · A classic in gremolata (with lemon zest and garlic) and tabbouleh salad.
  · A key component in French fines herbes and bouquet garni.
  · Fantastic as a fresh garnish on almost any savory dish to add a pop of freshness.
· Tip: Stems have a lot of flavor; chop them finely and use in marinades, stocks, or sauces.

3. Cilantro (Coriander Leaves)

· Flavor: Bright, citrusy, and pungent. (Note: For some people, it tastes like soap due to a genetic trait.)
· Common Uses:
  · Essential in Mexican salsas, guacamole, and tacos.
  · Found in Asian dishes like curries, pho, and chutneys.
  · A staple in Indian chutneys and as a garnish for many dishes.
· Tip: Like basil, it's very delicate. Stir it in at the end of cooking or use it raw.

4. Rosemary

· Flavor: Piney, woody, and powerfully aromatic.
· Common Uses:
  · Perfect for roasting lamb, chicken, and potatoes.
  · Excellent in breads (like focaccia) and with hearty vegetables.
  · Can be used in marinades and infused oils.
· Tip: Use sparingly, as it can be overpowering. The leaves are tough, so chop them finely or use whole sprigs and remove before serving.

5. Thyme

· Flavor: Earthy, slightly minty, and lemony.
· Common Uses:
  · A cornerstone of French cuisine, found in bouquet garni and herbes de Provence.
  · Wonderful in stews, soups, braises, and with roasted meats, poultry, and vegetables (especially mushrooms and onions).
· Tip: The leaves are small and usually stay on the stem. You can add whole sprigs to slow-cooked dishes and remove the stem later, or "strip" the leaves by running your fingers down the stem.

Other Incredibly Common & Useful Herbs

6. Oregano

· Flavor: Pungent, earthy, and slightly bitter. Dried oregano is more potent than fresh.
· Common Uses: Essential for Italian-American cuisine (pizza, pasta sauces), Greek cuisine (salads, meat seasoning), and Mexican salsas.

7. Mint

· Flavor: Cool, sweet, and refreshing.
· Common Uses:
  · Savory: In Middle Eastern dishes (like tabbouleh, lamb), Vietnamese spring rolls, and Indian chutneys (raita).
  · Sweet: In desserts, cocktails (mojitos), and teas.

8. Dill

· Flavor: Delicate, grassy, and slightly anise-like.
· Common Uses: Classic with fish (gravlax), potatoes (in potato salad), in tzatziki sauce, and in many Scandinavian and Eastern European dishes.

9. Sage

· Flavor: Earthy, slightly peppery, and musky.
· Common Uses: Traditionally paired with pork and in poultry dishes (like Thanksgiving stuffing). A key ingredient in brown butter sauce (burro e salvia) for pasta.

10. Chives

· Flavor: A mild, delicate onion flavor.
· Common Uses: Perfect as a garnish for potatoes, soups, salads, and deviled eggs. A member of the French fines herbes blend. Use them raw or add at the very end of cooking.

Quick Guide: Fresh vs. Dried

· Delicate Herbs (use fresh): Basil, Cilantro, Parsley, Dill, Mint, Chives. Their subtle flavors are lost when dried.
· Woody Herbs (can use dried): Rosemary, Thyme, Oregano, Sage. Their robust flavors hold up well to drying.

General Conversion: 1 tablespoon fresh herbs ≈ 1 teaspoon dried herbs.

Pro Tips for Using Herbs

1. When to Add Them: Woody herbs (rosemary, thyme) can be added early in cooking to infuse their flavor. Delicate herbs (basil, cilantro, parsley) should be stirred in at the very end to preserve their fresh taste and color.
2. Storage: Treat fresh herbs like a bouquet of flowers. Trim the stems and place them in a glass of water in the fridge, loosely covered with a plastic bag.
3. Don't Discard Stems: Herb stems (especially from parsley and cilantro) are packed with flavor. Use them to flavor stocks, soups, or marinades, then remove before serving.
